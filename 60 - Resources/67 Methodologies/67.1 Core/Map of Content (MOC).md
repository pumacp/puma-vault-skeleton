# Map of Content (MOC)

> A curated index note that gathers related notes into a navigable map — a hand-made table of contents for an emerging topic.

## How it works
A MOC is just a note whose job is to *link to other notes*. Two common shapes:

- **Index MOC** — a stable directory for a mature topic (e.g. `MOC-Methodologies`).
- **Workbench MOC** — a living scratch-map for a topic you are actively developing; notes graduate off it as they mature.

MOCs give the bottom-up Zettelkasten a layer of top-down navigation without forcing a rigid hierarchy. The vault keeps them in `80 - MOC`.

## Source
Nick Milo, "Maps of Content" / Linking Your Thinking (LYT) framework.

## Bridge
- **Zettelkasten** supplies the atomic notes; the MOC curates them into a topic. See [[Zettelkasten]].
- **Johnny Decimal** gives each MOC a stable address so it can be cited and embedded. See [[Johnny Decimal]].
- Provides the **Brainstorm** surface in the CODE cycle. See [[CeReBro (CODE)]].

## Worked examples
- **Example 1 — A "Thesis Writing" MOC.** Scattered notes on outlining, citation, and revision are linked from one new MOC, turning a pile of notes into a guided path a newcomer can follow top-to-bottom.
- **Example 2 — Index vs workbench.** While a topic is in flux you keep a *workbench* MOC with rough groupings and open questions; once it stabilizes you rewrite it as a clean *index* MOC and archive the workbench notes.
